**Why this matters to you.** A coding agent will hand you code that looks right and is subtly wrong, and the cost lands on you: reviewing a plausible numerical kernel line by line is often slower than writing it yourself. The way out is not a smarter model — it is a verifier the agent's output must pass before anyone trusts it. On 2026-06-11, [OpenAI published an account](https://openai.com/index/using-codex-to-simulate-black-holes) of Chi-kwan Chan, an astrophysicist at the University of Arizona and a member of the Event Horizon Telescope (EHT) collaboration — the team behind the first image of a black hole in 2019 — who works exactly this way with Codex (OpenAI's coding agent) in his research, as the team moves toward the first video of the supermassive black hole at the center of the M87 galaxy.

**What Chan actually does.** His bottleneck is plasma — superheated matter made of charged electrons and ions — near supermassive black holes. In hot, diffuse regions the particles [rarely collide and instead spiral around magnetic field lines](https://openai.com/index/using-codex-to-simulate-black-holes), so a standard simulation must compute every tiny turn at extremely small timesteps; even the fastest supercomputers spend most of their time on those corkscrews. "For decades, this has limited how realistically we can simulate black hole plasma," [Chan said](https://openai.com/index/using-codex-to-simulate-black-holes). He uses Codex to derive and implement candidate numerical algorithms that mathematically change how particle motion is tracked, so the simulation no longer follows every spiral directly. [Many of the generated approaches fail](https://openai.com/index/using-codex-to-simulate-black-holes). What makes the workflow safe is that every candidate is inspectable, and each one is tested against problems with known solutions before it is accepted.

**The idea underneath.** Chan's argument is that [science suits AI precisely because ideas are accepted only after rigorous verification](https://openai.com/index/using-codex-to-simulate-black-holes) — an algorithm earns its place by passing checks, not by looking convincing. That property is not specific to astrophysics. When a task has a cheap, trustworthy oracle — a known-correct answer the output must reproduce — you can let an agent generate aggressively, tolerate a high failure rate, and let the test decide what survives. Where no oracle exists, a human becomes the verifier, and agent throughput collapses to review throughput. Keep the source's hedge in view: if the new algorithms work, they [could enable simulations of trillions of particles](https://openai.com/index/using-codex-to-simulate-black-holes) — that is the hoped-for payoff, not a delivered result.

**What to do tomorrow.** Pick one task you already delegate to a coding agent and write the known-answer check before the prompt: a test whose correct output you can state independently — an analytic solution, a golden file, a brute-force reference implementation. Wire it into the agent's loop so failed candidates are discarded without you reading them. That is [Chan's setup](https://openai.com/index/using-codex-to-simulate-black-holes): generation is allowed to be wrong often, because verification is the gate.
