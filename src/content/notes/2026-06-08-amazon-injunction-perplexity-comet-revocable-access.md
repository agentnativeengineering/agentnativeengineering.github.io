**Why this matters to you.** An agentic browser — software that takes a loose goal like "buy a toaster under $100" and clicks through a site on your behalf — usually works by riding *your* logged-in session, reusing the cookies and credentials already in your browser. That feels safe: the user said yes, and the user owns the account. The wall you hit is that the *platform*, not the user, still controls authorization, and it can revoke it out from under your agent. On 2026-06-06, the Technology & Marketing Law Blog [published an analysis](https://blog.ericgoldman.org/archives/2026/06/when-can-amazon-block-an-agentic-ai-service-amazon-v-perplexity-guest-blog-post.htm) of the *Amazon v. Perplexity* injunction, where exactly that happened — Amazon told Perplexity's Comet agent to stop, Comet kept shopping through users' accounts, and a court shut it down.

**What the court did.** On March 9, 2026, [Judge Chesney granted Amazon a preliminary injunction](https://blog.ericgoldman.org/archives/2026/06/when-can-amazon-block-an-agentic-ai-service-amazon-v-perplexity-guest-blog-post.htm), finding Amazon likely to win under the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA), the U.S. federal anti-hacking statute, and California Penal Code section 502. The chain the court drew was simple: Amazon "allegedly revoked authorization, Perplexity's agents continued accessing Amazon's systems through user accounts, and therefore Amazon was likely to succeed." Comet was logged in as a real user, with that user's blessing — and per the ruling, that was not enough to keep its access authorized once Amazon sent the cease-and-desist and Comet [didn't stop](https://blog.ericgoldman.org/archives/2026/06/when-can-amazon-block-an-agentic-ai-service-amazon-v-perplexity-guest-blog-post.htm).

**The idea underneath.** An agent's authorization to touch a system is not the same thing as the user's permission to use their own account. The platform grants the human access under its terms, and the guest blogger argues the court treated the agent as "just another unwanted logged-in integration," leaning on older scraping precedent rather than asking whether [an agent is like a browser, a delegated assistant, or something new](https://blog.ericgoldman.org/archives/2026/06/when-can-amazon-block-an-agentic-ai-service-amazon-v-perplexity-guest-blog-post.htm). He calls that a dodge of the genuinely novel question. But the operational fact for you today is narrower and harder: borrowed authority is revocable authority, and a platform's "no" can turn your agent's logged-in traffic into "unauthorized access."

**What to do tomorrow.** If you run a browser or shopping agent against a third-party platform through a user's session, treat the platform's authorization — not the user's intent — as the thing that can cut you off, and wire a cease-and-desist or terms-of-service revocation in as a real production kill switch. Detect the block (a sudden 403, a challenge page, an account flag) and degrade gracefully instead of retry-looping into a system that has explicitly revoked you, which is the behavior that [put Comet under injunction](https://blog.ericgoldman.org/archives/2026/06/when-can-amazon-block-an-agentic-ai-service-amazon-v-perplexity-guest-blog-post.htm).
